Strategic frame.

v2 · 3 decisions from the 12 May team call

The v1 script was structured around 9 speakers across 9 scenes. For v2 we restructured around the 5 rubric areas judges actually score on — so they can match our content to their scoring sheet line by line. The 12 May team call locked the changes below.

Decision 01

9 → 5 restructure.

Judges tick 5 rubric scores, not 9. The new spine is one scene per rubric (Identify · Design · Create · Iterate · Communicate). All 9 of us still speak — distributed 1–2 per scene with lead + backup coverage.

Decision 02

Exceeds-trigger strategy.

Score 4 = "Exceeds" requires a written judge comment. Every scene now has one quotable line so memorable the judge can't help writing it. Target: 3 of 5 at Exceeds. Stretch: all 5.

Decision 03

Five physical evidence folders.

Inspired by last year's Malaysian winners. One A4 folder per rubric, handed to judges so they have proof in hand while scoring that rubric. Recommended timing: one folder per scene (5 micro-handovers).

The bar

If we land 3+ Exceeds out of 5, Robot Design rank should be top 3 minimum, top 1 likely.

Combined with the Robot Performance score that already wins, Robot Design becomes the Champion's Score multiplier — not the gap.

Scene 01 Identify Mission strategy + resources
TBD TBD · backup 60 sec 120 words

Five strategies. Four rejected. 8-run plan adopted.

Lead points to the animated mission strategy map as they name the rejected strategies. Backup hands Folder 1 (Identify) to the judges at the start.

Spoken · draft
We had 27 tasks. 15 missions. Two objectives: minimise runs, maximise points, lowest failure probability. We considered 5 strategies. We rejected 4. One run for all 15 missions — rejected, one failure loses all 545 points. 15 individual runs — rejected, too much time in Home. 5 to 6 runs with bigger attachments — rejected, attachments destabilised the robot. Group purely by proximity — rejected, efficient on path, slow on missions. We chose 8 runs grouped by three factors: proximity, sector sweeping (left-to-right so Kilo pieces don't block our path), and similar attachments together. To get here we followed five teams — Exploding Kettles, RailTech, Bricks and Biscuits, FLL Tutorials, Spike Prime Lessons — plus SPIKE Prime tutorials, Pybricks documentation, and YouTube on gear ratios and torque. (points to the animated 15→8 map) That's our 8-run plan.

Scene 02 Design 5-generation robot evolution

World Dominator is our 5th-generation robot. Each version fixed the previous one's failure mode. (point left-to-right) Generation 1 — Spike Prime hub-only base. Width-reduced TrackX, thick rubber tyres 62 millimetres by 20, low centre of gravity, gyro-controlled straight-line drive, four motors — two for driving, two for attachments — plus a wheel cover system built by the whole team after Submerged Run 8 jammed. One reliable system.

Scene 03 Create Attachments + code
TBD TBD · backup 60 sec 120 words

15 missions sound like 15 problems. They're not.

Lead demonstrates with the modular attachments on the table. Backup shows the custom menu screen + hands Folder 3 (Create) to the judges.

Spoken · draft
15 missions sound like 15 different problems. They're not. We tested every attachment we built and saw a pattern. Five mechanisms handle every mission. Grabbers. Push-pull arms. Rack-and-pinion. Dual-motor arms. Rubber band systems. (demonstrates with the modular attachments) We stopped engineering missions. We engineered mechanisms. One mechanism, multiple missions, predictable behaviour. For the code, we switched from SPIKE block coding to Pybricks for three reasons: precise distance control, custom wheel-diameter calibration (needed because we changed to 62×20mm wheels), and no Bluetooth lag. We built a custom menu that auto-increments runs — one button press loads the next. The gyro sensor handles straight-line drift, and our mechanical aligners and starting jigs make every launch fail-safe.

We test until we trust. Year 1, our runs were 40% consistent. Year 4, we're at 95%. That isn't luck. Why this script blows judges off.

5 mechanics most teams don't combine
M1

Rubric-aligned structure.

5 scenes = 5 rubrics. The judge knows exactly which row they're scoring at any moment. Last year the Malaysian winners did this. It's not score-chasing — it's making the judge's job easier.

M2

One Exceeds-trigger line per scene.

5 quotable lines, designed to force the judge's hand into the comment box. Most teams give judges nothing to write and get 2s and 3s. We give them the comment on a platter.

M3

Numbers anchor every scene.

27, 15, 545, 5, 4, 5, 4, 5, 220, 100, 50+, 40 → 95, 62×20, 502, 96. Korean judges, English second language — follow numbers faster than rhetoric.

M4

Lead + backup speaker per scene.

If the lead misses the Exceeds-trigger line, the backup catches it. 9 kids covering 5 lines = redundancy. Every kid in a scene knows their scene's trigger line even if they're not the one saying it.

M5

The meta-reframe at slot 5.

The slot-5 line — "Our robot isn't our best engineering. We are" — reframes everything the judges just heard. Engineering at the team-design level is rare even in adult engineering. From children, it's the line judges remember.

Rehearsal protocol.

6 weeks · don't memorise verbatim

Memorise the structure and the key lines. The exact wording will move slightly between rehearsals — that's fine. The structure must not.

Week 01 Allocate speakers (lead + backup per scene), then each speaker memorises their own section. Practice in isolation. Hit time targets within 5 sec.
Week 02 Run the full script as a team. Focus on scene-to-scene handoffs (lead → lead). Time the full thing. Aim for 4:30 to 4:45.
Week 03 Drill the slot-5 reveal in isolation 20 times. The line that has to land perfectly. Pace, eye contact, pause before "We are."
Week 04 Full run with artefact movements. Practice the awkward gear changes — robot handovers, folder handovers. These are the moments where teams stumble visibly.
Week 05 Full run in front of an external audience — parents, other students. Get feedback. Adjust.
Week 06 Polish only. No structural changes. Two full runs per day. Last week before Korea.
