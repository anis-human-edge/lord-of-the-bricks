Prep · Gracious Professionalism playbook

The behaviour that wins the rooms we're not in.

Three Gracious Professionalism scores feed Core Values directly. They're observed in the pit, at the tables, and in interactions with volunteers. Every kid memorises the moves before we walk into the venue.

Pit zone — our base camp.

GP1 of 3

The pit is a public space. Other teams walk past. Volunteers check in. Kids from other delegations stop to look at our robot. Treat the pit as a judging surface.

Do

  • Greet every volunteer who approaches by introducing yourself first.
  • Keep the pit visibly tidy — banners up, table clear, no rubbish.
  • Invite curious kids from other teams to look at the robot.
  • Have a kid on "ambassador duty" at all times — the others can rest.
  • Thank teams that lend tools / parts / batteries — by name.

Don't

  • Eat lunch on the pit table.
  • Let any kid look at their phone in the pit during open hours.
  • Speak negatively about other teams or other robots — even quietly.
  • Crowd around the table when only one or two kids need to work.
  • Let a parent answer for a kid when a visitor asks a question.

Tables zone — match conduct.

GP2 of 3

Referees watch the table. Other teams' parents watch the table. Cameras watch the table. Robot Game scoring is pure points — but the conduct around the points is what feeds Core Values.

Do

  • Shake hands with the opposing team and the ref at the start and the end.
  • Celebrate visibly but quickly — a high-five, then back to the next mission.
  • If the robot fails, recover calmly. The recovery is more visible than the failure.
  • Help the opposing team lift their robot off the table if they need it.
  • Thank the table referee by name at the end of the match.

Don't

  • Argue with referees on the spot. Note the issue, raise it later, do it calmly.
  • Celebrate over the opposing team's misfortune.
  • Blame each other in earshot of anyone outside the team.
  • Walk away from the table without packing up the area.
  • Show frustration in body language — judges read posture as much as words.

Volunteer + peer interactions.

GP3 of 3

The catch-all. Anything we say to a stranger in a lanyard, anything we do in a queue, anything other kids overhear in a corridor.

Do

  • Let other teams go first when there's a queue and we're not in a rush.
  • Compliment specific things on other teams' work, by name. Not generic.
  • Offer to help any team that's clearly struggling with hardware.
  • Thank by name. Not "thanks" — "thanks, [name]".
  • Speak to volunteers as equals, not as adults vs kids.

Don't

  • Joke about another team in a corridor — sound carries.
  • Take selfies with judges or volunteers without asking first.
  • Use the venue wifi for unrelated games — the network is monitored.
  • Speak ill of any other delegation's country or culture, ever.
  • Let any single kid carry the social weight of the team alone.

The 5 hard rules.

Memorised by every kid before we land in Korea
  1. Phones away in the pit and at the tables, full stop.
  2. Thank by name — every volunteer, every helpful kid, every referee.
  3. If we lose, we walk to the winning team and congratulate them within 60 seconds.
  4. If we win, we name the team that pushed us hardest in our acceptance.
  5. Leave every space cleaner than we found it.
