Prep · Judge psychology & Exceeds triggers

How judges decide — and the artefacts that decide them.

Two non-obvious leverage points carry most of the weight in this rubric. This page maps both, and lists the specific artefacts we need on the table to win them.

The two leverage points.

Leverage 01

The Exceeds column requires a written judge comment.

Score 4 ("Exceeds") on any rubric row is the only score that demands the judge write something down. They will not write something down for an abstract claim. They write when there's a quotable line in the script or a visible artefact on the table.

No artefact = no Exceeds.

So our job is to engineer one quotable line and one visible artefact per rubric row across IP and RD. 20 rows total. That's the artefact table below.

Leverage 02

The Final Share & Feedback slot is up to 8 minutes.

It's where most teams collapse — they treat it as small talk after the "real" judging. It's also where Core Values and Gracious Professionalism get cemented in the judges' final impression of us.

We weaponise this slot.

Fully scripted. Beat-by-beat. Specific people named. Specific failures admitted. Specific other teams credited. See the Final Share script.

Judge psychology — patterns to remember.

P1
Specificity scores higher than effort.

"We worked really hard on this" is invisible. "We tested 47 times and the success rate went from 62% to 88%" is unforgettable. Replace every effort claim with a number or a name.

P2
Honesty is the rarest signal in the room.

Most teams sanitise. Teams that name a failure, name what broke, name what they'd do differently, score above teams that present perfection.

P3
Crediting peers is almost free Exceeds.

Almost no team names another team in their Final Share. Doing so triggers Gracious Professionalism cleanly and is impossible to ignore.

P4
The walk in and the walk out are scored.

Judges form their first impression in the first 8 seconds. They form their final impression in the last 4. Treat both like presentations.

P5
Each kid speaking once = inclusion observed.

If only the loudest kid talks, the rubric scores it. Hard rule: no slide goes by where the same kid speaks twice in a row.

P6
Asking the judges a question is rare and powerful.

Ending a presentation with a genuine question to the panel inverts the dynamic and almost always generates a long, written, quotable comment from the judge.
