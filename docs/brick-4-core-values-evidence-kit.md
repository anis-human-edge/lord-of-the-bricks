## The scoring surface

Core Values is not a separate session. It is scored on:

- **10 dual-marked rows** hidden inside the Innovation Project and Robot Design rubrics (5 from each), each scored 1 to 4
- **3 Gracious Professionalism scores** observed across the whole event (pit, tables, common areas)

Champion's Score = Innovation Project Rank + Core Values Rank + Robot Design Rank + Robot Game Rank.
Lowest total wins. Target: rank 1 in all four = score of 4.

Core Values is 25% of the Champion's Score. Half of that is earned inside the IP and RD presentations. The other half is earned in real time at the event.

## The 3 GP scores: observed at the event

These are not scored in the judging room. They are scored by volunteers watching the team across the entire event. Three observation zones:

1. **Pit zone:** how the team behaves at its own station, with neighbours, with visitors
2. **Table zone:** how the team behaves at the practice table and the competition table, between matches
3. **Common areas:** lunch, queues, opening / closing ceremonies, with volunteers and referees

Telling the pay-it-forward story to the judges does NOT score these 3 points. The team has to DO it visibly.

---

## GP Playbook for Korea

### Day 1 morning (highest leverage)

Within the first hour of pit setup:
1. Walk the pit. Identify 2 to 3 teams that look the youngest or most nervous.
2. Approach one team. Lead with a specific compliment (their robot colour, their team shirt, something concrete).
3. Offer one small, useful thing: a sticker, a UK-themed pin, a snack to share.
4. Ask one question about their season. Listen.
5. Make a return visit later in the day.

### Daily standing rules

- Read every volunteer and referee name tag. Use their name.
- Thank every volunteer at every interaction. Even the obvious ones.
- Cheer at the practice table for at least one non-UK team every day.
- If something goes wrong with your robot at the table: never blame. Always thank the referee.
- Wait quietly for queue. Never crowd another team's practice table.
- Pick up trash that isn't yours, at least once a day, where volunteers can see.

### Specific Korea moves

- Learn 5 Korean phrases before departure:
  - 안녕하세요 (Annyeonghaseyo) = Hello
  - 감사합니다 (Gamsahamnida) = Thank you
  - 화이팅 (Hwaiting) = Good luck / Go for it
  - 축하해요 (Chukhahaeyo) = Congratulations
  - 잘 했어요 (Jal haesseoyo) = You did great
- Carry small UK gifts to exchange with Korean teams (London postcards, UK-flag pins, small Lego figures).
- If the K-pop hook is 100% sharp by 2 weeks out: perform it as a friendship gesture for the team next door on Day 1.
- If it isn't sharp: scrap the dance, keep the slogan chant.

### Quotable line for when volunteers ask why

> "We were helped in our first year. Now in our fourth year, we pay it back. Especially to first-time teams."

## Final Share and Feedback script (8 minutes)

This is the highest-leverage closing slot in the entire judging session. Most teams collapse here. Use it.

**Structure (8 min total):**

**Opening (30 sec) — Esha**
> "Thank you for letting us share our season. Before we finish, four things we want to leave you with."

**Block 1: Most proud of (2 min) — Arvin or Alia (a new member)**
The pay-it-forward story. Specific team. Specific score. Before and after.
> "What we're most proud of isn't on the table or in the AI. Three years ago, in our first FLL season, an older team came over to our table. They congratulated us. Then they showed us a few simple things. We doubled our score that day. This season at Regionals, we found a primary school team that had just scored 150 points. We went to their table. We congratulated them. We showed them the same things we'd been shown. We don't know their final score, but we know we closed a loop. We want to keep doing it. Especially here in Korea."

**Block 2: Toughest challenge (2 min) — Anaisa or Elliot**
The decision-making process for the Innovation Project.
> "Our toughest challenge wasn't technical. It was deciding what problem to solve. We spent nearly half the season exploring options. LiDAR for site mapping. Social media for archaeology outreach. A funding portal for archaeologists. Each of us pitched. After two to three months of research, MVPs, and conversations, we made a simple choice: stop inventing, start listening. We asked the Kent Archaeological Society what their biggest problem actually was. Their answer became our project."

**Block 3: How the coaches helped (1.5 min) — Sakthi or Toby**
> "Our coaches are our parents. They are not engineers. They are project managers. They didn't write the code. They drove us to interviews. They hosted us every Sunday for the full season. They kept our schedule across 96 sessions. When we were stuck, they didn't give us answers. They helped us figure out how to find the answer ourselves. We built the AI. They built the environment."

**Block 4: Conflicts (1 min) — Flossie**
> "We've been friends since primary school. We rarely disagree about people. We disagree about ideas. Our rule: test instead of debate. When we couldn't decide between a rack-and-pinion mechanism and a passive rod, we built both and tested under the same conditions. The data made the decision. That became our protocol for the rest of the season."

**Block 5: Closing identity (1 min) — All 9**
- Team slogan (TBC — Akash to provide)
- Optional K-pop hook (only if 100% sharp)
- Final chant, all together, eye contact with judges: **"WE ARE LORD OF THE BRICKS!"**

Reserve last 30 sec for clarifying questions.
