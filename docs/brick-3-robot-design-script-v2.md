## Key decisions we took

### Decision 1 · Restructure from 9 speakers → 5 rubrics

**Old structure (v1):** 9 scenes, one per speaker, ~30 seconds each. Speaker-organised.

**New structure (v2):** 5 scenes, one per rubric area, ~60 seconds each. Rubric-organised. All 9 kids still speak — they distribute across the 5 scenes with 1–2 speakers per scene.

**Why we changed:**
- Judges score on **5 rubrics** (Identify · Design · Create · Iterate · Communicate). They tick 5 boxes total, not 9.
- The old 9-scene structure was organised around the **people**, not the **rubrics**. The judges had to figure out which content went into which rubric while listening.
- The new 5-scene structure **matches what the judge is grading on**. They know exactly which rubric we're in at any moment.
- Per Lo: *"in fact in the first regionals that's how we did and the judges did say at the end 'oh you helped us — you covered everything'."*
- **Backup coverage**: each scene has 2 speakers. If one speaker misses the Exceeds-trigger line, the other catches it. Nine kids covering 5 trigger lines = redundancy.

**Concern raised on the call** (Anis + Lo): could structuring this obviously around the 5 rubrics make us look like we're "scoring the marks" rather than telling a story? **Verification open with RealTechBot** — but the Malaysian team that won last year did it exactly this way, so we're leaning yes.

---

### Decision 2 · Engineer 2–3+ "Exceeds Expectations" attempts

The whole script is now designed around landing **at least 2–3 Exceeds (4-out-of-4) scores** — with a stretch goal of all 5. Every scene has **one specific trigger line** designed to be so quotable that the judge can't help writing it in the comment box.

**Why we changed:**
- Score 4 in any rubric **requires** a written judge comment.
- Most teams don't give the judges anything memorable to write, so they default to 2 or 3.
- The Real TechBot coach guidance: *"Force them into writing the comment on the Exceeds. You have to give them that comment on a platter so that they write it."*
- Tutorials we watched repeatedly say: **no memorable artefact = no Exceeds**.

**Coverage strategy:** every kid in a scene knows what their scene's trigger line is, even if they're not the one saying it. If the lead speaker misses or rushes past it, the backup speaker delivers it. We rehearse this redundancy deliberately.

---

### Decision 3 · Five physical evidence folders (one per rubric)

We will hand the judges **5 A4 ring-bound folders**, each titled with its rubric name, each containing the hard evidence for that rubric.

**Why we changed:**
- The Malaysian team that won the international last year did exactly this — and it worked.
- Judges can refer to the folders while scoring **that specific rubric**, instead of having to remember everything we said.
- Gives us a clear way to back up every claim with physical proof.
- Lo: *"They will be listening to you but they know you are a strong team because you produced some solid evidence to them before even starting."*

**Open question — timing of the handover:**
- **Option A (Malaysian model):** all 5 folders handed at the very start of the presentation.
- **Option B (Adjoa's view):** never hand anything at the start — distraction factor breaks the presentation flow.
- **Option C (compromise, recommended):** hand the relevant folder at the **start of each rubric** — 5 small handovers, none disruptive, each folder reaches the judge just before they score that rubric.
- **VERIFY with RealTechBot before locking.**

### Decision 4 · One simple visual per scene (5 total, not 9)

The visual behind the speaker is single-purpose per scene. Each visual must be understandable in **under 5 seconds**.

**Why we changed:**
- 9 visuals would be impossible to keep simple.
- Anis: *"If it is even little bit complex that people are going to look at will switch off."*
- Lo: *"The aim of the animation is to simplify the understanding."*
- Hand-drawn is fine if it's clear. Slick is not the goal — **clarity** is.

**The 5 visuals:**
1. Identify → animated 15-mission map collapsing into 8 runs, with 4 rejected strategies fading below as crossed-out cards.
2. Design → 5 robots in a row with pros/cons + tick/cross per generation.
3. Create → 5 mechanism categories on the left, custom menu screen on the right.
4. Iterate → the 40% → 95% consistency curve printed large, nothing else.
5. Communicate → sub-group diagram with all 9 names + 502 hours / 96 sessions overlay.
